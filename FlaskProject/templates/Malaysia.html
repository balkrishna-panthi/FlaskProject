Malaysia

Malaysia is a federal constitutional monarchy located in Southeast Asia, comprising 13 states and three federal territories divided into two regions: Peninsular Malaysia and East Malaysia. The country shares land borders with Thailand, Brunei, and Indonesia, and maritime borders with Singapore, Vietnam, and the Philippines. Kuala Lumpur serves as the national capital and largest city, while Putrajaya is the administrative center. With a population exceeding 34 million, Malaysia is the 43rd most populous nation globally and is known for its tropical climate and rich biodiversity, being one of 17 megadiverse countries. Historically, Malaysia originated from the Malay kingdoms and became a British protectorate in the 18th century. After World War II and a brief unification as the Malayan Union in 1946, it was restructured into the Federation of Malaya, gaining independence on August 31, 1957. On September 16, 1963, Malaya united with North Borneo, Sarawak, and Singapore to form Malaysia, although Singapore was expelled from the federation in August 1965.

Culture

Malaysia's culture is a rich tapestry woven from the traditions of its diverse ethnic groups, including indigenous tribes, Malays, and immigrants from China and India. Influences from Persian, Arabic, and British cultures further enrich this cultural landscape, resulting in a unique blend of identities that coexist and sometimes overlap.

Attractions

Malaysia is a vibrant Southeast Asian destination that offers a unique blend of Malay culture and natural diversity, attracting tourists of all ages with its notable attractions, including the Twin Towers, Batu Caves, Astana, and Legoland.

Cuisine

Malaysia's cuisine is a reflection of its multiethnic population, showcasing a blend of influences from Malay, Chinese, Indian, Thai, Javanese, and Sumatran cuisines due to its historical position along the spice route. While many dishes retain their cultural origins, they often evolve with local adaptations, leading to unique flavors and styles, such as the sweeter versions of Chinese dishes and the distinct cuisine of the Peranakans, who combine Chinese techniques with Malay ingredients.
